**why i went from "ethan" to "ethan yizheng" to simply "yizheng"**
<br>
Feb 24, 2023, 21:40

before i start i wanted to thank the teachers who respected my transition and called me accordingly :) yet i still feel the need to address *why* such a change was necessary for me. i haven't even set up my website properly so this clumsy Markdown file will do.

> TL;DR: i changed my English name to just the pinyin of my Chinese name because (a) i wanted to stand out from my peers while protecting my name from being treated as a disposable, supplementary name and (b) i want to challenge the idea, the notion that Chinese pinyin cannot constitute as a legit name and you must adopt an English name no matter what, and that coming from a non-English speaking culture is to be ashamed of and shoved away in the corner as far as you can.
>
> (also i don't like sharing my name with people i don't like (yeah you probably figured my thinking was still as immature as ever lol)
>
> (*aside*: after writing all of this i realized that i didn't actually talk about why i used "ethan" and "yizheng" jointly XD but basically i was giving myself the illusion that my name was more unique than it really is (and also i heard that was what Asian--Americans do...))

i've been struggling with my identity, trying to find a balance between my English self and Chinese self. i have few friends but none of them make me feel truly myself, and conflicts with my parents only pushed me further to the edge. i indulge in the superiority of being the only person among my peer group to consider English my mother tongue, but i also find myself chastising my cowardly behavior to run away from people instead of telling them how i truly feel.

it also bothered me -- no, *infuriated* me -- how it is common and even expected it is to adopt an English name. the name "ethan" followed me since my childhood and to this very day, ever since i was to join an English class when i was 6 or 7 years old. yet it somehow had never struck me how common it is to have this name.... and as i became increasingly uncomfortable with the number of people i came to discover who have this name, i began to feel more and more strongly as if ethan wasn't my name...

this feeling also worsened over time when i filled out forms for AP exams, contests, summer program, etc. -- i always wrote yizheng as my first name. and i used to so eagerly wait for the day i can change my legal name and shift my Chinese name to the middle, but it all changed one day when i had a shift in perspective...

long story short, i stopped seeing my Chinese pinyin as an illegitimate cheap transliteration, but an *actual name*... that represented who i am, beyond just random letters i never looked back upon once i am done doing impersonal legal business with them.

it still is hard to accept this fully, but i can say i am learning to. i was first to call myself this, followed by a subtle name change on my school's grading system requested by me, as well as changing my nicknames in all the group chats i considered pertinent to me. what i didn't expect, though, was that some teachers noticed that change and started calling me yizheng instead of ethan, which -- i am more than glad to hear! (unless it is said in a family name--first name order; that is something i won't go into here...) of course, none of my peers called me yizheng, and most people seem to be more acquainted with "ethan" instead (despite with the knowledge of the *other* Ethan at our school..... >:c)


whether i will keep to this change might depend on how i feel in the future, but what is certain is that my identities are finally merged into ONE... and it felt FUCKIN' AWESOME. before it felt as if my peers and i maintained a separate persona to deceive our non-Chinese teachers, and i felt so terrible that we were hiding our true persona we actually use in daily lives and on formal occasions behind a mask... (you can probably see why i hate this expectation: no one but our own people (who ironically don't speak English) set up this social expectation to have a conventional western name, as if our pinyin, however more authentic and easy-to-pronounce, is cheap and something to used as a last resort when you can't find a name for yourself under a nice list of English names.)

i know i'll never be an American, a British, or anything like that. i'll remain a Chinese, no matter how hard i try to blend in with all the white people -- those who speak real, legit English -- it still needs to be proven in the eye of college admission officers through language tests like TOEFL. if my only name IS legally and legitimately yizheng, then why bother trying to find some other obscure, possibly weird names? fearing that my peers might refuse to catch on? well... i'll never talk to them again anyways. (actually, if they *did* catch on i might just get a bit worried -- really if anything this separates my true self and a facade: this is the use of deception i actually would gladly agree with! XD (i just really don't like my classmates, hehe...))

on a lighthearted note (possible scornful too, sorry... ;P), it also feels like a teasing remark to most Chinese people who seem to unanimously agree on picking an English name -- however common or even ridiculous it may be. on a more practical note, i'd say that i want to use my case to *challenge* this belief people seem to have: that in order to put your name around some English words you'd have to "translate" it too somehow (you don't, just use pinyin unless the pinyin is easy to mispronounce), that if you don't have an English name then everyone wouldn't have a name to call you when talking in English (i've spent years hoping to get a less common English name, yet i was too blind to see that my Chinese name IS the unique name i've been pressured to never use.)

with all these doubts of who i am that i still have, my action as of now is to just put it to use and see how people would react, and also see how i'd react: is this something a Chinese person would do? are pinyin names really an indiscernible mess that kills its original meaning? (i bet if you looked at the name "ethan" you wouldn't immediately think of how it means "strong; firm" in Hebrew. so does that mean you must be reminded of how my name yizheng comes from the Chinese characters "以正"?) must a Chinese person be bad at English, speak with a strong accent, and are they less so if they chose to use an English name in place of their Chinese name? and even if they do have an accent, is it something to be frowned upon? do we have to force every person from a different culture to assimilate, or is it better to simply accept that not everyone speaks "perfect" English, and that "imperfection" is what makes one's culture, one's identity, more pronounced?

at best i want to get some fellow Chinese people into thinking about this and, potentially reaching the conclusion that they'd prefer to not adopt an English name, like many of our Asian neighbors, the Japanese, the Korean, etc. (i'm not familiar with other Asian countries sorry 💀) at the moment i like both my Chinese and English name, but the indoctrinating belief that you're nothing without your English name is so infuriating i can't help but abandon my English name. :))))))) i surely do not want to come of as treating either one of the names lightly, and given the number of people who have Ethan as their one and only legal name, i don't want to make their name feel generic and throwaway either (not that i can distort statistics, but i can certainly try helping with the attitude...) if people here respected English names and treated them as complete and legit, that's another story, but since they don't, i am just gonna... regghh, bleghh, fuck it, screw it, i'd rather say that ethan isn't my name if it ain't treated like one.
